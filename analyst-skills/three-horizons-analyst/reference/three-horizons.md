# Three Horizons of Growth — Reference

## The three horizons

| Horizon | What it is | Primary metric | Mindset |
|---|---|---|---|
| **H1 — Core** | Mature, profitable businesses that are today's engine | Profit, margin, efficiency, cash | Defend and extend |
| **H2 — Emerging** | Fast-growing businesses with proven demand, not yet profitable at scale | Revenue growth, share, scaling milestones | Invest to build |
| **H3 — Options** | Pilots, research, seeds of future businesses | Learning, validated assumptions, option value | Experiment cheaply |

All three are managed *concurrently*. The discipline is funding and governing each by its own logic.

## Maturity, not calendar time

The common error is mapping horizons to fixed time windows (0–2 / 2–5 / 5+ years). Horizons are about **maturity and business-model proof**, not the clock. A fast-moving market can have an H3 idea become H1 in 18 months; a slow one can have H2 businesses scale for a decade. Classify by:
- Is demand proven? Is the model profitable? Is it scaling or steady?

## Hand-off conditions (where pipelines die)

Pipelines fail at transitions, not within a horizon. Make the gates explicit:
- **H3 → H2:** the option has validated demand and a credible path to a repeatable model. Graduate it and fund scaling.
- **H2 → H1:** the business is profitable at scale and stable; shift metrics from growth to efficiency.

State, for the focal business, the one hand-off most at risk of stalling.

## Portfolio-balance diagnostic

- **Over-weighted H1:** strong current profit, thin H2/H3 — the company is harvesting and will face a growth cliff. Common in incumbents.
- **Over-weighted H3:** many bets, starved core — exciting but cash-fragile. Common in over-funded challengers.
- **Healthy:** a defended core funding a real H2 with a seeded H3 behind it.

Apply H1 profit metrics to an H3 option and you will kill it before it can prove itself — flag any such governance mismatch.

## Market vs. company horizons

Run the horizons twice:
1. **The company's portfolio** — where its initiatives sit.
2. **The market's evolution** — which of today's profit pools (H1) are eroding, which new spaces (H2) are forming, which disruptions (H3) loom.
A company whose H1 sits in an eroding market pool faces urgency the portfolio view alone won't show.

## Common errors

- Equating horizons with fixed time buckets.
- Judging H3 by near-term revenue.
- Letting H1's culture and metrics govern H2/H3.
- Treating the framework as three separate plans rather than one balanced portfolio with hand-offs.

- **H3 (options)** `[INFERENCE] 0.4`: automated micro-fulfillment robotics and a returns-handling platform — pilots only; judge by learning.

**Balance read** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: over-weighted to a *declining* H1; H2 is real but under-funded relative to the core's erosion. Urgency is higher than the P&L suggests because the H1 pool itself is shrinking.
