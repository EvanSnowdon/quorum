# 7 Powers — Reference

## The benefit-and-barrier test

A **Power** exists only when BOTH columns are satisfied *relative to a specific competitor*. A benefit without a barrier is a transient advantage, not a power.

| Power | Benefit (improves cash flow via…) | Barrier (why the competitor can't / won't match) |
|---|---|---|
| **Scale Economies** | Lower unit cost as volume rises | Challenger would need comparable share; the cost of buying that share exceeds the prize |
| **Network Economies** | Higher willingness-to-pay / lower cost as the user base grows | Challenger cannot match installed base; value gap compounds |
| **Counter-Positioning** | A superior, often lower-cost business model | Incumbent **declines to copy** because it would cannibalize its existing profits (collateral damage) |
| **Switching Costs** | Higher prices / retention; customers locked to the value already invested | To win a customer the rival must compensate the switching cost — uneconomic |
| **Branding** | Durable price premium from affective trust | Equivalent reputation takes years and is uncertain to acquire |
| **Cornered Resource** | Preferential access to a coveted asset (talent, patent, deposit, deal) on attractive terms | The rival simply cannot obtain it |
| **Process Power** | Lower cost or higher quality from embedded organizational routines | Hysteresis — even if observed, replication is slow, complex, and tacit |

## Intensity × durability rubric

For each power *that passes the test*, score two axes:

- **Magnitude (m̄)** — how much it widens the margin gap vs. the rival. Rough bands: small (<5 pts), moderate (5–15 pts), large (>15 pts of differential margin).
- **Durability** — expected years the barrier holds before erosion (technology shift, regulation, taste change, scale parity). State the erosion mechanism, not just a number.

A real moat usually rests on **one or two** strong powers, not a long checklist of weak ones.

## Origination: when powers are *established*

Powers are created in specific windows, not declared:
- **Scale / Network** — established as a market takes off and share concentrates.
- **Counter-Positioning** — established when a newcomer adopts a model the incumbent rationally won't follow.
- **Cornered Resource** — established at the moment of preferential access (a hire, a license, a deal).
- **Switching Costs / Branding / Process** — accrue with the installed base and time.
Ask: *did a window exist for this firm, and did it act in it?* If no window was seized, claimed power is aspirational.

## The "not a power" list (reject these unless they convert to one of the seven)

- "First mover" — only matters if it converted into scale, network, switching costs, or a cornered resource.
- "Great product / better tech" — sustaining quality the rival can match is not a barrier.
- "Strong management," "great culture" — only a power if it manifests as Process Power.
- "High market share" — a *result*, not a power; ask what barrier produced and protects it.
- "Patents" — a Cornered Resource only while they block the rival and matter economically.

## Common errors

- Listing benefits and skipping the barrier test (the most frequent mistake).
- Assessing power in the abstract instead of *against a named competitor*.
- Confusing industry attractiveness (Five Forces) with firm-level power.
- Over-counting: claiming five weak powers instead of identifying the one that actually drives returns.

## Worked example (≈170 words)

**Focal:** Leading B2B accounting-software vendor, {region} SME segment. **Rival:** a well-funded cloud challenger.

- **Switching Costs — strong** `[INFERENCE] 0.75`: migrating live ledgers, integrations, and trained staff is costly and risky; the rival must subsidize the full switch. Benefit ✓ (retention, pricing power), Barrier ✓ (compensation uneconomic). Durability ~5–7 yrs.
- **Network / ecosystem — moderate** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: accountant-referral and add-on marketplace; rival's smaller ecosystem widens the gap slowly. Benefit ✓, Barrier ✓ (partial).
- **Branding — moderate** `[DATA] 0.6`: trusted-of-record status among accountants; hard but not impossible to copy.
- **Scale Economies — weak** `[INFERENCE] 0.5`: cloud marginal cost is low; share matters less for unit cost. Benefit ✓, Barrier ✗ → **not yet a power**.
- **Counter-Positioning — risk** `[INFERENCE] 0.55`: if the challenger's usage-based pricing is one the incumbent's seat-license P&L can't adopt without self-harm, the incumbent is the *exposed* party here.

**Powers held:** switching costs (primary), ecosystem network + branding (supporting). **Durability: 4/5.** **Erosion path:** a counter-positioned pricing model the incumbent can't match without cannibalizing seat-license revenue.
