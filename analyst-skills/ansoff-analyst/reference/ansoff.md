# Ansoff Growth Matrix — Reference

## The grid

| | Existing products | New products |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing markets** | Market penetration (lowest risk) | Product development (moderate risk) |
| **New markets** | Market development (moderate risk) | Diversification (highest risk) |

"Existing" vs "new" are defined relative to the focal business's current position — fix that baseline before placing any move.

## Quadrant playbooks

**Market penetration** — grow within the known:
- Win share from competitors
- Increase usage frequency / basket size
- Improve retention and win back churned customers
- Tactical pricing and promotion
- Lowest risk; first place to look for growth.

**Market development** — same product, new buyers:
- New geographies (regions, countries)
- New customer segments (e.g., consumer → SMB)
- New channels (retail → direct, offline → online)
- Risk: misreading new-market needs, regulation, distribution.

**Product development** — new product, same buyers:
- Line extensions and adjacent products
- Bundles and complements
- Next-generation replacements
- Risk: R&D execution, cannibalization, time-to-market.

**Diversification** — new product *and* new market:
- Related: leverages existing technology, channel, brand, or customer knowledge
- Unrelated (conglomerate): little operational overlap; relies on financial logic
- Highest risk; demands the clearest justification.

## Risk ladder

Risk increases with distance from current competencies and customers:

penetration < market development ≈ product development < related diversification < unrelated diversification

Always pair the placement with the *reason* for the risk in {region} (e.g., "market development into a neighboring country carries regulatory and brand-recognition risk").

## Related vs unrelated diversification test

Ask what is shared between the new venture and the core:
- Technology / production?
- Distribution channel?
- Customer base or brand?
- Management know-how?

Two or more strong overlaps → related (manageable). None → unrelated (treat as a near-standalone bet and demand a financial-portfolio rationale).

## Feasibility checklist (per vector)

- Capabilities required vs. held (and buildable in what time?)
- Capital intensity and payback
- Competitive intensity in the target space
- Cannibalization of existing revenue
- Regulatory / channel access

## Common errors

- Listing four quadrants as if equally available; ignoring that penetration is usually cheaper and surer.
- Labeling a move "diversification" without classifying related vs unrelated.
- Treating a new channel as a new product (it's market development).
- Ignoring cannibalization in product development.

## Worked example (≈160 words)

**Business:** A regional craft-brewery selling bottled beer through grocery in {region}.

- **Penetration** `[INFERENCE] 0.7` (low risk): grow share in current grocery via more facings, seasonal SKUs, loyalty — headroom exists if current share is modest `[DATA]`.
- **Market development** `[INFERENCE] 0.6` (moderate): enter the on-trade (bars/restaurants) channel, or a neighboring region — same product, new buyers; needs distribution build-out.
- **Product development** `[INFERENCE] 0.6` (moderate): launch a non-alcoholic line for existing grocery customers — rides the same brand and shelf, R&D-light.
- **Diversification — related** `[INFERENCE] 0.4` (high): branded brew-pub hospitality (new product, new market) sharing brand and customer knowledge; capital-heavy.

**Ranked shortlist:** 1) penetration (secure the base), 2) product development (NA line — cheap adjacency), 3) market development (on-trade). **Recommended first move:** market penetration, because it is lowest-risk and the share data show room before any stretch is warranted.
