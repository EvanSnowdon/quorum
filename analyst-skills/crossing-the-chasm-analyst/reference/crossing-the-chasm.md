# Crossing the Chasm — Reference

## The five adopter groups (each buys for a different reason)

| Group | a.k.a. | Buys because… | References / trusts | Tolerance for an incomplete product |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Innovators** | Techies / enthusiasts | The technology itself is interesting | Other techies | Very high — will tinker |
| **Early adopters** | Visionaries | A strategic leap / competitive breakthrough | Their own vision; analysts | High — expect to assemble pieces |
| **Early majority** | Pragmatists | A proven productivity improvement, low risk | **Other pragmatists in their industry** | Low — want a complete, supported solution |
| **Late majority** | Conservatives | It's now the safe standard; price matters | Established players, the herd | Very low — want it turnkey and cheap |
| **Laggards** | Skeptics | Forced to; embedded in something else | No one (block purchases) | None |

The two psychographic gaps that matter: the small cracks between groups, and the **chasm** — the deep gap between **early adopters (visionaries)** and **early majority (pragmatists)**.

## Why the chasm exists

Visionaries and pragmatists want opposite things, and **pragmatists will not take a visionary as a reference**. So early sales success (to visionaries) generates *no* useful momentum with the mainstream. Revenue stalls. Mistaking visionary traction for mainstream readiness is the classic failure.

## Chasm diagnostic (is this the binding obstacle?)

Likely a chasm if:
- Early sales clustered among visionaries/innovators; mainstream penetration flat.
- Prospects ask for references "from companies like us" and there are none.
- Deals stall on "is it complete / proven / supported?" rather than price or features.
- The product still requires the buyer to integrate or assemble pieces.

Not a chasm (look elsewhere) if growth is slow due to price, distribution, regulation, or a genuinely continuous (non-behavior-changing) product.

## Beachhead-selection scorecard (the target-customer "bowling pin")

Pick ONE segment that maximizes:
- **Compelling reason to buy** — a must-solve, budgeted pain (not a nice-to-have).
- **Single, identifiable economic buyer / role** — you can name the person.
- **Whole-product feasibility** — you (and partners) can deliver a complete solution to *this* segment soon.
- **Word-of-mouth density** — the segment members talk to each other (so references propagate).
- **Adjacency** — winning it knocks over neighboring segments next (the bowling-pin chain).
- **Beatable competition** — no entrenched incumbent already owning the segment.

Concentrate everything on dominating that one segment. Spreading across segments = falling into the chasm.

## Whole-product map

Pragmatists buy the *whole* product, not the generic one. Map the gap:

- **Generic product** — what you actually ship.
- **Expected product** — the minimum the buyer assumes (basic support, docs).
- **Augmented product** — integrations, services, training that fulfill the use case.
- **Potential product** — ecosystem, third-party add-ons.

List who supplies each layer for the beachhead (you, partners, channel). Unfilled layers = adoption blockers.

## Positioning / value-proposition template

> **For** [target beachhead customer] **who** [compelling reason to buy / need],
> **our product is a** [market category] **that** [key benefit / why buy],
> **unlike** [the competitive alternative], **our product** [key differentiation].

Pragmatists need the **market category** (so they can file you mentally) and the **competition** (so they know what they're choosing instead of).

## Common errors

- Treating visionary sales as proof of mainstream demand.
- Attacking many segments at once ("the product is horizontal!") — guarantees no reference base forms.
- Shipping the generic product to pragmatists who need the whole product.
- No named economic buyer for the beachhead.
- Applying the model to a continuous innovation that doesn't change behavior (no chasm exists).

## Worked example (≈180 words)

**Product:** Electronic health-records (EHR) module for independent dental clinics, {region}.

- **Discontinuous?** `[INFERENCE] 0.7` Yes — requires workflow change and supporting integrations.
- **Lifecycle placement** `[DATA] 0.6`: adopted by a few tech-forward chains (visionaries); penetration among independent clinics ~low single digits. **Pre-/at-chasm.**
- **Chasm diagnosis** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: independents (pragmatists) ask for peer references and a turnkey, billing-integrated solution; current product needs assembly → stalled.
- **Beachhead** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: **independent multi-chair dental clinics in [one metro]**; economic buyer = practice owner-dentist; compelling reason = insurance-claim rejections cost them money; high word-of-mouth via local dental associations; adjacency to neighboring metros.
- **Whole product** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: generic = the EHR app; gaps to fill = local insurance-claim integration, data migration service, and on-site training (deliver via a local IT partner).
- **Positioning:** *For independent dental clinics who lose revenue to rejected claims, [product] is a clinic-management system that gets claims paid first time, unlike generic EHRs that leave billing to you.*

**Stage:** pre-chasm. **Chasm is the binding obstacle:** yes. **Beachhead:** independent multi-chair dental clinics in one metro, sold on first-time claim approval.
