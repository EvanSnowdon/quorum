# Five Forces — Reference

## Rating rubric (per force)

Rate each force by its strength as a *threat to incumbent profitability*.

| Rating | Meaning | Typical signals |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Force exerts little downward pressure on profit | High barriers, fragmented counterparties, no credible substitute, benign rivalry |
| **Medium** | Force constrains profit but is manageable | Mixed signals; some leverage on each side |
| **High** | Force is a serious or binding drag on profit | Low barriers, concentrated counterparties, strong substitutes, or destructive rivalry |

Always pair the rating with the two or three drivers that justify it. A rating without drivers is unfalsifiable.

## Driver checklists

**Threat of new entrants (High = easy entry):**
- Supply-side economies of scale; demand-side network effects
- Capital requirements and sunk costs
- Customer switching costs; incumbency advantages independent of scale (brand, IP, location, learning curve)
- Access to distribution channels
- Restrictive government / regulatory policy (licenses, standards)
- Expected incumbent retaliation (history of price wars, deep pockets, slow growth)

**Supplier power (High = suppliers capture value):**
- Supplier concentration vs. the industry
- Reliance on a particular input; availability of substitute inputs
- Switching costs to change suppliers
- Credible forward-integration threat
- The industry's importance to the supplier group's revenue

**Buyer power (High = buyers capture value):**
- Buyer concentration; volume per buyer
- Standardized / undifferentiated product
- Low switching costs for buyers
- Credible backward-integration threat
- Price sensitivity (input cost as a share of buyer's budget; buyer profitability)

**Threat of substitutes (High = substitutes cap pricing):**
- Relative price-performance of out-of-industry alternatives
- Buyer propensity to substitute; switching costs to the substitute
- Watch adjacent technologies and changing buyer behavior, not just like-for-like products

**Rivalry (High = competition erodes profit):**
- Numerous or equally balanced competitors
- Slow industry growth (share is zero-sum)
- High fixed or storage costs; perishability
- Lack of differentiation; low switching costs
- High exit barriers (specialized assets, emotional/strategic stakes)
- Diverse competitor objectives (e.g., state-backed players not maximizing profit)

## Common boundary-definition errors

- **Too broad** — "the food industry" instead of "chilled ready-meals in {region}". Forces wash out to Medium and the analysis says nothing.
- **Too narrow** — defining the industry around one firm's product so substitutes vanish.
- **Confusing complements with forces.** Complements (e.g., apps for a console) raise or lower demand but are not a sixth force here; note them under attractiveness, not as a force.
- **Treating government as exogenous noise.** In regulated or state-influenced markets, regulation shows up *inside* entry barriers, rivalry (state-backed competitors), and buyer/supplier power — not as a separate box.

## Worked example (≈150 words)

**Industry:** Independent fitness studios (boutique gyms), urban {region} market.

- **Threat of new entrants — High** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: low capital to open a single studio, no scale economy, abundant commercial real estate; minimal retaliation between small players.
- **Supplier power — Low** `[DATA] 0.6`: equipment and landlords are fragmented; staff (instructors) are the real input and can be a Medium pressure where star trainers move clientele.
- **Buyer power — High** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: members face near-zero switching costs and face many substitutes; month-to-month contracts dominate.
- **Substitutes — High** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: home fitness apps, chains, and outdoor exercise all cap pricing.
- **Rivalry — High** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: dense competition, weak differentiation, churn.

**Binding constraint:** buyer power × substitutes. **Overall attractiveness: 2/5** — structurally hard to earn above-cost-of-capital returns without a differentiation or community moat.
