# Playing to Win — Reference

## The strategy choice cascade (worksheet)

Five linked choices. Each row both *follows from* the one above and *reinforces* it.

| # | Choice | The question | Test of a good answer |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Winning aspiration** | What is our purpose; what does winning look like, for whom, against whom? | Names customers to delight and competitors to beat — not a generic mission |
| 2 | **Where to play** | On what field? (geography, segment, channel, category, value-chain stage, vertical/horizontal) | Defined by explicit *exclusions*; you can name where you will NOT play |
| 3 | **How to win** | How do we create unique sustainable value *there*? | Specifies the basis of advantage (cost OR differentiation) and *why customers choose us* |
| 4 | **Capabilities** | What set of reinforcing activities must we have/build? | A *system* of 3–6 mutually-supporting capabilities, not a wish list |
| 5 | **Management systems** | What systems, structures, measures support and sustain the capabilities? | Concretely builds and renews the capabilities (talent, metrics, IT, planning) |

## How-to-win: cost vs. differentiation decision aid

| | Cost leadership | Differentiation |
|---|---|---|
| Source of advantage | Lowest delivered cost; price or margin headroom | Buyer-valued uniqueness commanding a premium / loyalty |
| Where-to-play fit | Price-sensitive, standardized segments; scale available | Segments with unmet needs and willingness to pay |
| Capability emphasis | Process, scale, supply chain, simplification | Brand, innovation, service, design, customer intimacy |
| Failure mode | "Stuck in the middle" — neither lowest cost nor differentiated | Premium customers won't pay for; uniqueness rivals copy |

A how-to-win that says "better quality at a competitive price" is usually *stuck in the middle*. Force the choice.

## Coherence test (read the cascade both ways)

- **Top-down:** does where-to-play serve the aspiration? Does how-to-win work on that field? Do the capabilities deliver the how-to-win? Do the systems build the capabilities?
- **Bottom-up:** do the management systems and capabilities actually *enable* the stated how-to-win, or are they generic? A capability that any rival also has is not a source of winning.
- **Reinforcement:** the strongest strategies have capabilities that reinforce *each other* (an activity system), making the whole hard to copy.

## "What would have to be true?" (reverse-engineering)

Instead of debating whether a strategy *is* right, list the **conditions that would have to be true** for it to be the best choice — across industry attractiveness, segment value, relative cost/differentiation position, capabilities, and competitor response. Then:
1. Sort conditions by **how confident** you are in each.
2. Test the **least confident, most consequential** one first.
3. That condition is the strategy's make-or-break assumption — state it explicitly.

## Common errors

- Treating the five choices as five independent slides instead of one cascade.
- A where-to-play with no exclusions ("everyone, everywhere").
- A how-to-win that is an aspiration ("be the best") rather than a mechanism.
- Capability lists that are generic ("great people, great tech") rather than a reinforcing system.
- Skipping management systems, so capabilities are asserted but never built.

## Worked example (≈190 words)

**Player:** challenger grocery retailer, {region} urban market.

1. **Winning aspiration** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: be the first-choice weekly shop for value-seeking urban families, beating full-range supermarkets on price-per-trip.
2. **Where to play** `[DATA] 0.7`: dense urban catchments; ~1,500-SKU limited-assortment stores; private-label-led; **excludes** rural, hypermarket formats, and long-tail national brands.
3. **How to win** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: **cost leadership** — lowest delivered cost via a narrow SKU count, high volume per line, and private label; pass savings to price. Customers choose it because the basket is reliably cheaper.
4. **Capabilities** `[INFERENCE] 0.65`: ruthless SKU rationalization; private-label sourcing scale; lean store ops; fast-turn supply chain — a reinforcing low-cost system.
5. **Management systems** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: supplier scorecards, like-for-like cost metrics, simplified labor model, category-captain data.

**Coherence:** strong — each layer reinforces low cost; capabilities form a system rivals can't cheaply copy without abandoning their full-range model (a counter-positioning angle). **Make-or-break assumption** `[INFERENCE] 0.55`: that enough urban families will trade range for price at the scale needed to hit volume-per-SKU economics. Test segment price-elasticity and basket data first.

**One-liners:** Aspiration: default value weekly shop. Where: dense urban, limited assortment, private-label. How: lowest delivered cost. Capabilities: SKU discipline + sourcing + lean ops. Systems: supplier scorecards + cost metrics. **Coherent.** **Risk:** range-for-price trade-off at scale.
