# JTBD & Disruption — Reference

## Job-statement template

> When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].

Capture three dimensions:
- **Functional** — the practical task to accomplish.
- **Emotional** — how the customer wants to feel (confident, unburdened).
- **Social** — how the customer wants to be perceived by others.

A good job statement is solution-agnostic: it does not name a product, and it stays true even as technology changes. "I need a quarter-inch hole" is closer; "I need a drill" is a solution.

## The competing set (by job, not by category)

List every alternative hired for the job, in three buckets:
1. **Direct** — products in the same category.
2. **Indirect / workaround** — different categories, hacks, manual methods.
3. **Non-consumption** — the customer does nothing, or cannot afford / access any solution.

New-market disruption almost always starts in bucket 3.

## Disruption litmus test

A challenger is **disruptive** (not merely competitive) when most of these hold:

- It enters at the **low end** (customers the incumbent overserves and is happy to lose) **or** in a **new market** (former non-consumers).
- Its initial offering is **worse on the attributes the mainstream prizes** but good enough on a different basis (price, convenience, simplicity, access).
- It rides an **enabling technology or business model** that improves over time on the mainstream attributes.
- The incumbent has an **asymmetric motivation to flee upmarket** rather than fight — defending the low end would dilute its margins.
- Over time the challenger's improvement **trajectory intersects** mainstream requirements, and customers switch.

If the entrant simply makes a better product for the incumbent's best customers, it is a **sustaining** innovation — label it as such, not as disruption. Incumbents usually win sustaining battles.

## Low-end vs new-market disruption

| | Low-end | New-market |
|---|---|---|
| Initial customers | Overserved existing customers | Non-consumers |
| Initial value prop | Good enough, cheaper | Access / simplicity where none existed |
| Incumbent reaction | Happy to cede low margins | Doesn't see them as a market |
| Example pattern | Discount/basic tier eats the bottom | A simpler product creates a new tier of users |

## Overshoot signal

The industry has overshot when the mainstream stops paying for further improvement (features go unused, NPS plateaus despite "better" products, price sensitivity rises). Overshoot is the precondition for low-end disruption.

## Common errors

- Calling any fast-growing or well-funded rival "disruptive."
- Defining the market by product category, hiding non-consumption.
- Ignoring the incumbent's incentive asymmetry — the theory is about *why incumbents rationally fail to respond*, not just about cheap entrants.

## Worked example (≈160 words)

**Industry:** Retail tax-preparation services, {region}.

- **Job** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: "When my taxes are due and I'm unsure of the rules, I want to file correctly without anxiety, so I can avoid penalties and feel in control." (functional: file; emotional: peace of mind; social: be a responsible adult.)
- **Competing set** `[DATA] 0.6`: storefront preparers, accountants, DIY software, and large-scale **non-consumption** (people who delay, guess, or never file).
- **Disruption read** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: low-cost guided software entered at the **low end** (simple returns the storefronts overserved) and in a **new market** (non-filers), good-enough and far cheaper, improving yearly toward complex returns.
- **Incumbent asymmetry** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: high fixed-cost storefronts cannot profitably chase simple returns, so they cede them.

**Disruption risk: high.** Most exposed position: storefront preparers serving simple returns. Indicator to watch: the share of moderately complex returns migrating to software each season.
