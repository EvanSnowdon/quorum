# Value Chain — Reference

## Activity checklist

**Primary activities (the line that makes and delivers the offering):**

| Activity | What to characterize |
|---|---|
| Inbound logistics | Receiving, warehousing, inventory control of inputs |
| Operations | Transforming inputs into the finished offering; yield, utilization |
| Outbound logistics | Storing and distributing to buyers; reach, speed |
| Marketing & sales | Demand generation, channel, pricing, brand |
| Service | Installation, support, returns, lifecycle revenue |

**Support activities (cut across the whole line):**

| Activity | What to characterize |
|---|---|
| Firm infrastructure | Management, finance, planning, quality systems |
| HR management | Recruiting, training, retention of scarce skills |
| Technology development | R&D, process tech, product tech, IT |
| Procurement | Sourcing of inputs (the *function*, present in every primary activity) |

## Margin-pool worksheet

For each stage, fill in:

1. **Revenue or value added** at this stage (or the industry's split of end-customer price).
2. **Cost base** at this stage (and its main drivers).
3. **Implied margin** = value added − cost.
4. **Who controls it** — concentration; number of viable players.
5. **Replicability** — how hard to enter or imitate this stage.

The profit pool is rarely uniform. A common pattern: a low-margin manufacturing middle flanked by high-margin design/IP upstream and brand/service downstream (the "smile curve"). State where {industry} sits on that curve.

## Advantage drivers

**Cost advantage at a stage comes from:**
- Scale and capacity utilization
- Learning / experience curve
- Linkages (one activity lowering another's cost)
- Location, vertical integration, timing, policy/subsidy
- Disciplined procurement

**Differentiation advantage at a stage comes from:**
- Product features and quality buyers will pay for
- Brand and reputation
- Service, customization, responsiveness
- Linkages that competitors cannot easily coordinate

A stage is attractive to *occupy* when it both pools margin and is hard to replicate.

## Common errors

- Listing activities without estimating economics — a labeled diagram is not an analysis.
- Treating procurement as one box rather than a function inside every primary activity.
- Ignoring linkages, where much durable advantage actually lives.
- Confusing high revenue with high margin (assembly is often high-revenue, low-margin).

## Worked example (≈160 words)

**Industry:** Specialty-coffee retail, {region} urban market (value-system view).

- **Green-bean sourcing** `[DATA] 0.6`: commodity-linked, thin margin, many farmers; controlled upstream by traders. Low capture for retailers.
- **Roasting** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: moderate margin; differentiation possible via craft and freshness; moderate barriers (equipment, skill).
- **Operations (café)** `[DATA] 0.7`: largest cost block is rent + labor; utilization (seats × turns) drives unit economics.
- **Marketing & brand** `[INFERENCE] 0.7`: where premium pricing is earned; brand and ambience let a cup sell at a multiple of bean cost.
- **Service / loyalty** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: repeat visits and app-based loyalty lift lifetime value cheaply.

**Margin pools** in brand + in-store experience downstream, not in beans. **Buildable advantage:** differentiation via brand and consistent operations; cost advantage is hard given fragmented rent and labor markets. Chokepoint to watch: premium real estate locations.
