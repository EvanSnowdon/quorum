# Blue Ocean — Reference

## Strategy canvas (construction)

1. **X-axis:** the 6–10 factors the industry currently competes on and invests in (e.g., price, range, service, ambience, speed, brand). Use the factors *buyers* experience, not internal functions.
2. **Y-axis:** offering level, low → high (how much each player delivers on that factor).
3. **Value curves:** plot each major competitor / strategic group. Where curves overlap = competitive convergence = red ocean.
4. Read the canvas: *Where is everyone the same? Where is everyone over-serving? What does no curve touch?*

## ERRC grid (the Four Actions)

| Reduce cost | Lift buyer value |
|---|---|
| **ELIMINATE** — which factors that the industry takes for granted should be removed entirely? | **RAISE** — which factors should be raised well above the industry standard? |
| **REDUCE** — which factors should be reduced well below the standard (over-served)? | **CREATE** — which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? |

Left column drives **cost down**; right column drives **value up**. Doing both simultaneously = **value innovation** (the break in the value–cost trade-off). A grid that only *adds* (Raise/Create) without *removing* (Eliminate/Reduce) is feature creep, not blue ocean.

## Six paths to reconstruct market boundaries

1. **Across alternative industries** — products with different forms/functions serving the same goal (cinema vs. restaurant for "a night out").
2. **Across strategic groups** — between premium and budget tiers within the industry.
3. **Across the chain of buyers** — purchaser ≠ user ≠ influencer; redefine who you target.
4. **Across complementary offerings** — solve the total-solution pain before/around the product.
5. **Across functional ↔ emotional appeal** — flip an emotional industry to functional, or vice versa.
6. **Across time** — external trends with a clear, irreversible trajectory.

## Three tiers of non-customers (where new demand lives)

| Tier | Who | Unlock |
|---|---|---|
| **First — "soon to be"** | Minimally use the offering, ready to jump ship | Remove the reason they're about to leave |
| **Second — "refusing"** | Consciously choose against the category | Address why they opt out (price, complexity, fit) |
| **Third — "unexplored"** | Never considered; served by distant markets | Reframe so the category becomes relevant to them |

Blue oceans grow by converting non-customers, not by stealing existing share — quantify the pool.

## The three lever tests (does the new curve qualify?)

- **Focus** — the value curve emphasizes a few factors strongly, not many weakly.
- **Divergence** — the curve looks visibly *different* from the industry's, not a "me-too +5%."
- **Compelling tagline** — a true, sharp one-liner stating the offer. No tagline = no clarity.

## Strategic sequence (viability gate, in order)

1. **Buyer utility** — exceptional utility across the buyer-experience cycle (purchase, delivery, use, supplements, maintenance, disposal) × six utility levers (productivity, simplicity, convenience, risk, fun/image, environmental).
2. **Price** — strategic pricing set to capture the *mass*, not skim early.
3. **Cost** — can the ERRC hit the target cost at that price? (cost-led, not price-led.)
4. **Adoption** — resolve hurdles among employees, partners, and the public.
Fail any link → not yet a viable blue ocean.

## Common errors

- Adding features without eliminating/reducing anything (cost balloons; no value innovation).
- Treating it as pure differentiation — blue ocean requires *low cost too*.
- "Non-customers" asserted without sizing the pool.
- A canvas built on internal functions instead of buyer-experienced factors.
- Chasing a fad mislabeled as a "trend with a clear trajectory."

## Worked example (≈190 words)

**Industry:** Budget intercity travel, {region}. As-is canvas competitors: rail and full-service coach lines competing on price, comfort, station amenities, schedule frequency, on-board service. Curves converge on mid-price/mid-comfort.

**ERRC:**

| Eliminate | Raise |
|---|---|
| Ticket offices & travel agents (move 100% mobile) | Departure frequency on core city-pairs |
| On-board catering | Price transparency / cheapest-seat clarity |
| **Reduce** | **Create** |
| Comfort/legroom to "good enough" | Direct point-to-point routes bypassing hubs; dynamic ultra-low lead-in fares; self-service app |

**Value innovation** `[INFERENCE] 0.65`: strip cost (no offices, no catering, basic comfort) *and* raise value (frequency, transparency, direct routes) → differentiation at a structurally lower cost.

**Non-customers** `[INFERENCE] 0.6`: primarily **second-tier "refusing"** — people who currently drive or don't travel because rail/coach feels expensive and inconvenient. Size via household travel-survey data `[DATA] 0.5`.

**Lever test:** Focus ✓ (price + frequency + directness). Divergence ✓ (curve diverges from full-service). Tagline ✓.

**Strategic sequence:** utility ✓ (convenience, simplicity), strategic price set for the mass, cost target met by the ERRC, adoption hurdle = driver in cash-preferring segments.

**Move:** an app-only, no-frills, high-frequency direct coach network priced to convert people who currently drive. **Tier:** refusing non-customers. **Tagline:** "Cheaper than driving, simpler than the train."
